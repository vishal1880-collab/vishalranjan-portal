## What is income tax filing in India?

Income tax filing in India is mandatory for individuals with annual income above ₹2.5 lakh (₹3 lakh under the new regime), all companies and LLPs regardless of profit, and any entity claiming refunds or carry-forward of losses. The financial year runs **April 1 to March 31**, with returns due by **31 July** for non-audit cases and **31 October** for audit cases. Filing is fully online through the Income Tax e-filing portal.

## Who must file an income tax return?

Filing is mandatory if any of the following apply:

- Individual income above **₹2.5 lakh** (old regime) or **₹3 lakh** (new regime)
- Senior citizens (60–80 years) above **₹3 lakh**
- Super senior citizens (80+) above **₹5 lakh**
- All companies, LLPs, and partnership firms — regardless of income
- Any person with foreign assets or foreign income
- Deposited **₹1 crore+** in current accounts during the year
- Spent **₹2 lakh+** on foreign travel
- Paid **₹1 lakh+** in electricity bills
- Want to claim a **refund** or **carry forward losses**

## Old regime vs new regime: which is better?

**New tax regime (default from FY 2023–24):**

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|-------------|----------|
| Up to ₹3 lakh | Nil |
| ₹3–7 lakh | 5% |
| ₹7–10 lakh | 10% |
| ₹10–12 lakh | 15% |
| ₹12–15 lakh | 20% |
| Above ₹15 lakh | 30% |

Standard deduction of ₹75,000 applies. No deductions for 80C, HRA, LTA, etc.

**Old tax regime:**

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
|-------------|----------|
| Up to ₹2.5 lakh | Nil |
| ₹2.5–5 lakh | 5% |
| ₹5–10 lakh | 20% |
| Above ₹10 lakh | 30% |

Allows full deductions: 80C (₹1.5 lakh), 80D (health insurance), HRA, home loan interest, etc.

**Rule of thumb:** if your total deductions exceed ₹3.75 lakh, the old regime is usually better. Below that, the new regime saves tax.

|------|-----------|
| ITR-1 (Sahaj) | Salaried, income up to ₹50 lakh, one house property |

| ITR-7 | Trusts, political parties, research institutions |

## Documents required for ITR filing

- PAN and Aadhaar (linked)
- Form 16 from employer
- Form 16A / 16B / 16C for TDS on other income
- Form 26AS and AIS (Annual Information Statement)
- Bank statements for all accounts
- Investment proofs (80C, 80D, etc.)
- Capital gains statements (broker, mutual fund)
- Rent receipts and HRA details
- Home loan interest certificate
- Foreign income and asset disclosures (Schedule FA)

| Category | Due Date |

Late filing fee under section 234F: ₹5,000 (₹1,000 if income below ₹5 lakh). Interest under 234A at 1% per month on unpaid tax.

## Common income tax deductions

**Section 80C (₹1.5 lakh limit):** PPF, ELSS, life insurance premium, EPF, NSC, principal of home loan, tuition fees, 5-year tax-saver FD.

**Section 80D:** Health insurance premium — ₹25,000 self/family, ₹50,000 senior citizens.

**Section 80E:** Interest on education loan (no upper limit, 8 years).

**Section 80G:** Donations to approved charities — 50% or 100% depending on institution.

**Section 24(b):** Home loan interest — up to ₹2 lakh on self-occupied property.

**Section 80TTA / 80TTB:** Savings account interest (₹10,000 / ₹50,000 senior citizens).

**HRA exemption:** Lower of: actual HRA received, 50% of basic salary (metro) / 40% (non-metro), or rent paid minus 10% of basic.

## Penalties for non-filing or wrong filing

- **Late filing**: ₹5,000 (₹1,000 if income below ₹5 lakh)
- **Misreporting income**: 200% of tax sought to be evaded
- **Underreporting income**: 50% of tax sought to be evaded
- **Failure to file**: prosecution under section 276CC — 6 months to 7 years imprisonment plus fine
- **Concealment of foreign assets**: 300% penalty plus prosecution under Black Money Act

## Tax planning vs tax evasion vs tax avoidance

- **Tax planning**: legal use of deductions, exemptions, and structures to minimize tax. Encouraged.
- **Tax avoidance**: legally exploiting loopholes. Permissible but increasingly challenged under GAAR.
- **Tax evasion**: illegal concealment of income or false claims. Punishable.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the last date to file ITR for FY 2025–26?

31 July 2026 for individuals (non-audit) and 31 October 2026 for audit cases.

### What happens if I don't file ITR on time?

Late filing fee of ₹5,000 (₹1,000 if income below ₹5 lakh), interest at 1% per month on unpaid tax, and inability to carry forward losses.

### Should I choose the old or new tax regime?

The new regime is better if your total deductions are below ₹3.75 lakh. The old regime is better if you have significant 80C, 80D, HRA, and home loan deductions.

### Do I need to file ITR if my income is below the threshold?

Filing is mandatory if you have foreign assets, deposited ₹1 crore+ in current accounts, spent ₹2 lakh+ on foreign travel, or paid ₹1 lakh+ in electricity bills, regardless of income level.

### Can I revise my income tax return?

Yes, until 31 December of the assessment year. Beyond that, you can file an updated return (ITR-U) within 4 years from the end of the relevant assessment year, with additional tax of 25–60%.

### What is Form 26AS?

A consolidated tax statement showing TDS deducted, advance tax paid, and high-value transactions reported by banks and other entities. Always reconcile your ITR with Form 26AS and AIS.

### How long should I keep tax records?

Minimum 7 years from the end of the assessment year. For cases involving foreign assets, keep records indefinitely.
