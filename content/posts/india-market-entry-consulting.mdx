## What is India market entry?

India market entry is the process by which a foreign company establishes legal, financial, and operational presence in India. The four primary structures are **Wholly-Owned Subsidiary (WOS)**, **Joint Venture (JV)**, **Liaison Office (LO)**, and **Branch Office (BO)**. India received **$71 billion in FDI** in FY 2024–25, with most foreign entities entering through the **automatic route** (no prior RBI approval needed) for sectors covering 90%+ of the economy.

## Four primary entry routes for foreign companies

| Structure | Best For | Setup Time | Tax Status |
|-----------|----------|------------|------------|
| Wholly-Owned Subsidiary (Pvt Ltd) | Long-term operations, full control | 4–6 weeks | Indian company, 22–25% tax |
| Joint Venture | Local partnerships, regulated sectors | 6–10 weeks | Indian company |
| Liaison Office | Market research, no commercial activity | 8–12 weeks | No tax (no income) |
| Branch Office | Same business as parent, limited activities | 10–16 weeks | 40%+ tax on Indian income |
| Project Office | Specific project execution | 4–8 weeks | Project-based |
| LLP | Professional services, partnerships | 3–4 weeks | 30% flat tax |

For most operating businesses (SaaS, manufacturing, services, retail), a **Wholly-Owned Subsidiary** is the recommended structure.

## Wholly-Owned Subsidiary: the default choice

A WOS is a Private Limited Company incorporated in India where 100% shares are held by the foreign parent (subject to FDI sectoral caps).

**Advantages:**
- Full operational control
- Limited liability
- Permitted to undertake any commercial activity allowed under FDI policy
- Can repatriate profits, dividends, and royalties
- Independent legal entity

**Requirements:**
- Minimum **2 directors**, one of whom must be **Indian-resident** (≥182 days in previous year)
- Minimum **2 shareholders** (parent + 1 nominee, or parent + holding company)
- Registered office in India
- FDI compliance under FEMA
- RBI reporting via **Form FC-GPR** within 30 days of share allotment
- Annual **FLA return** (Foreign Liabilities and Assets) by 15 July

## FDI policy: automatic vs approval route

**Automatic route (90%+ of sectors):** No prior approval needed. Examples:
- IT and software services
- Manufacturing (most sectors)
- E-commerce (B2B and marketplace model)
- Renewable energy
- Pharmaceuticals (greenfield)
- Hotels and tourism
- Single-brand retail (up to 100%)

**Government approval route:** Requires DPIIT approval. Examples:
- Multi-brand retail trading (up to 51%)
- Print media
- Broadcasting (specified sub-sectors)
- Defence (above 74%)
- Pharmaceuticals (brownfield, above 74%)

**Prohibited sectors:** Lottery, gambling, chit funds, atomic energy, real estate trading (other than construction development), tobacco manufacturing.

## Liaison Office vs Branch Office vs Project Office

| Feature | Liaison Office | Branch Office | Project Office |
|---------|----------------|---------------|----------------|
| Permitted Activities | Representation, market research | Same as parent (limited) | Specific project only |
| Commercial Activity | Not allowed | Allowed | Allowed for project |
| Income Earning | Cannot earn income in India | Can earn from permitted activities | Project income |
| Approval | RBI / AD Bank | RBI / AD Bank | RBI / AD Bank |
| Validity | 3 years (extendable) | Indefinite | Project duration |
| Tax | Not taxed | 40% + surcharge + cess | Project-based |
| Net Worth Required (parent) | $50,000+ | $1,00,000+ | Project-specific |
| Profit Track Record | 3 years | 5 years | NA |

For most foreign companies, a **WOS is preferred over LO/BO** due to operational flexibility and lower compliance burden.

## Documents required from foreign parent

All foreign documents must be **apostilled** (for Hague Convention countries) or **notarized and consularized** (for non-Hague countries).

1. Certificate of Incorporation of foreign parent
2. Memorandum and Articles of foreign parent
3. Board resolution authorizing Indian subsidiary and signatory
4. List of directors and shareholders
5. Audited financials of last 3–5 years
6. Bank reference letter
7. ID and address proof of foreign directors and authorized signatory
8. Power of Attorney for Indian representatives

## Ongoing compliance for foreign-owned Indian entities

Beyond standard ROC and tax compliance:

- **Form FC-GPR** — within 30 days of share allotment to foreign investor
- **Form FC-TRS** — within 60 days of transfer between resident and non-resident
- **Annual FLA return** — by 15 July, reporting foreign liabilities and assets
- **APR (Annual Performance Report)** — for Indian companies with overseas investments
- **Transfer pricing study and Form 3CEB** — for international related-party transactions, due 31 October
- **Master File (Form 3CEAA)** and **CbC Report (Form 3CEAD)** — for groups with consolidated revenue above ₹500 crore / €750 million

## Repatriation of funds

Foreign-owned Indian companies can repatriate:

- **Dividends**: freely repatriable after DDT removal (FY 2020–21 onwards), subject to 20%+ withholding under treaty
- **Royalties and technical fees**: under automatic route up to specified limits
- **Capital**: on share buyback, capital reduction, or winding up — subject to RBI/AD bank reporting
- **Profits of LO/BO/PO**: as per Form 49C and FEMA compliance

DTAA benefits (treaty rates) apply to most countries — for example, **5%–10% on dividends** under most treaties vs 20%+ under domestic law.

## Common pitfalls in India market entry

1. **Wrong entry structure**: choosing LO when commercial activity is intended → forced restructuring later
2. **FDI route misclassification**: assuming automatic route in restricted sectors
3. **Late FC-GPR filing**: ₹5,000 + ₹500/day penalty under FEMA
4. **Resident director not appointed**: company cannot file forms
5. **Ignoring transfer pricing**: large adjustments and penalties
6. **Operating without GST when required**: penalty of 10% of tax + interest
7. **Apostille errors**: documents rejected, restart process
8. **Not opening bank account before share allotment**: capital can't be received

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the fastest way for a foreign company to enter India?

A Wholly-Owned Subsidiary (Private Limited Company) under the FDI automatic route. Typical timeline: 4–6 weeks. LLP is faster (3–4 weeks) but only suited for service businesses without equity fundraising plans.

### Does a foreign company need an Indian partner?

No, except in sectors with FDI caps below 100% (e.g., multi-brand retail, defence, certain media). Most sectors allow 100% foreign ownership under the automatic route.

### What is the minimum capital for a foreign subsidiary in India?

There is no statutory minimum paid-up capital. Practical minimum is ₹1 lakh – ₹10 lakh based on operational needs. Capital must be received through banking channels with FIRC documentation.

### How long does it take to repatriate dividends from India?

Dividends can be declared and paid as soon as profits are available and audited financials are finalized. Repatriation is generally completed within 7–15 days of board declaration, subject to AD bank documentation and tax clearance.

### Can a foreign director be the sole director?

No. At least one director of an Indian Private Limited Company must be a resident of India (≥182 days stay in the previous calendar year).

### What is FC-GPR and when must it be filed?

Form FC-GPR is the RBI filing for share allotment to foreign investors. It must be filed within 30 days of allotment. Late filing attracts compounding penalties under FEMA.

### Are profits of a Liaison Office taxable in India?

Liaison offices cannot earn income in India and are therefore not taxed on Indian operations. They are funded through inward remittances from the parent.

### What is the corporate tax rate for foreign-owned Indian subsidiaries?

22% (under Section 115BAA, no exemptions) or 25% (turnover up to ₹400 crore) plus surcharge and cess. Effective rates: 25.17% and 27.82% respectively. Branch offices are taxed at 40%+ on Indian income.
