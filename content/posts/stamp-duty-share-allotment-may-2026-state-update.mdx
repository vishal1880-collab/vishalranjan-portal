Stamp duty on share allotment is a state subject under Entry 63 of the State List in the Constitution of India, with the central Stamp Act providing a baseline framework that each state adapts through its own stamp legislation. The Indian Stamp Act, 1899 was amended by Finance Act 2019 to introduce uniformity for demat issues at 0.005 percent of the issue value, but physical certificate rates remain at state discretion and vary widely.

The Maharashtra rate of 0.005 percent on demat issues and 1 percent on physical certificates is the highest physical rate among major states and reflects the state's historical reluctance to reduce the physical rate to match the demat rate. The Maharashtra Stamp Act amendments have been under discussion since 2023 with industry representations seeking the rate be brought down to 0.1 percent for physical certificates, but no notification has been issued as of May 2026. The practical consequence is that companies headquartered in Maharashtra and issuing physical share certificates face substantially higher stamp duty cost than companies issuing through demat.

The Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, and other major commercial states have rationalised the physical certificate rate to 0.1 percent, which aligns closely with the demat rate when adjusted for transaction cost. Delhi has proposed a reduction from 1 percent to 0.1 percent but the notification has been pending since FY 2023-24, and the operative rate continues to be 1 percent for physical certificates. For Delhi-headquartered companies, the recommendation is to issue all new share allotments through demat to absorb the rate differential.

The payment workflow for stamp duty depends on whether the issue is in demat or physical mode. For demat allotments, the National Securities Depository Limited (NSDL) and Central Depository Services Limited (CDSL) collect the 0.005 percent stamp duty from the issuing company at the time of allotment crediting, and remit the duty to the state of registered office of the issuing company. For physical allotments, the payment is made directly to the state stamp authority through e-stamping (where the state has notified e-stamping) or through franking at a licensed stamp vendor.

The e-stamping migration has progressed substantially across states. Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Delhi, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and West Bengal all operate e-stamping for share allotments, with the Stock Holding Corporation of India Limited (SHCIL) being the central service provider for most states.

The Form SH-7 filing requirement under Section 39 of the Companies Act is the corollary corporate filing. SH-7 must be filed with the Registrar of Companies within 30 days of allotment, with details of allottees, amount paid, source of payment, and a copy of the resolution authorising the allotment. Late filing of SH-7 attracts ₹100 per day late fee with no upper cap.

For private placements under Section 42, the stamp duty and SH-7 filing workflow runs alongside the private placement compliance: PAS-3 within 15 days of allotment, the share allotment register update, and the share certificate issuance within 60 days of allotment under Section 56.
