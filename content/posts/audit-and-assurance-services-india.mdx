## What is an audit in India?

An audit in India is a systematic examination of a company's financial records, internal controls, and statutory compliance, conducted by a qualified Chartered Accountant (CA). Statutory audit is mandatory for **all companies** under the Companies Act 2013, **all LLPs with turnover above ₹40 lakh** or **capital contribution above ₹25 lakh**, and **businesses/professionals with turnover above ₹1 crore / ₹50 lakh** under Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act.

## Statutory audit: who needs it?

Mandatory for:
- **All companies** registered under Companies Act 2013 (Pvt Ltd, Public, OPC) — regardless of turnover
- **LLPs** with turnover above **₹40 lakh** or capital contribution above **₹25 lakh**
- **Co-operative societies** as per state laws
- **Trusts and societies** with income above specified thresholds

Auditor must be appointed within 30 days of incorporation (first auditor) and at every AGM thereafter for a 5-year term.

## Tax audit under Section 44AB

Mandatory for:

| Category | Threshold |
|----------|-----------|
| Business (cash transactions > 5%) | Turnover > ₹1 crore |
| Business (cash transactions ≤ 5%) | Turnover > ₹10 crore |
| Profession | Gross receipts > ₹50 lakh |
| Presumptive scheme (44AD) | Income claimed below 8% / 6% |
| Presumptive scheme (44ADA) | Income claimed below 50% |

Tax audit report (Form 3CA/3CB and 3CD) is due **30 September** of the assessment year. Late filing penalty: 0.5% of turnover, up to ₹1,50,000.

## GST audit and reconciliation

**GSTR-9C** reconciliation statement is mandatory for businesses with **aggregate turnover above ₹5 crore**. Due date: **31 December** of following financial year. Must be self-certified (since FY 2020–21) or CA-certified for departmental audits.

GST departmental audit under Section 65 can be conducted by tax authorities for any registered person, with 15-day prior notice. Period of audit cannot exceed one financial year.

## Internal audit

Mandatory under Section 138 of Companies Act for:
- All listed companies
- Unlisted public companies with paid-up capital ≥ ₹50 cr, turnover ≥ ₹200 cr, outstanding loans ≥ ₹100 cr, or deposits ≥ ₹25 cr
- Private companies with turnover ≥ ₹200 cr or outstanding loans ≥ ₹100 cr

Internal auditor reviews operational efficiency, risk management, fraud prevention, and compliance with internal policies.

## Audit process and timeline

A typical statutory audit follows this sequence:

| Phase | Activities | Duration |
|-------|------------|----------|
| Planning | Engagement letter, risk assessment, scope | 3–5 days |
| Interim audit | Review of internal controls, vouching | 5–10 days |
| Year-end audit | Verification of balances, confirmations | 7–15 days |
| Reporting | Draft report, management discussion, final report | 3–5 days |

Total duration: **3–6 weeks** for SMEs, longer for larger entities.

## What auditors check

A statutory audit verifies:

1. **Truth and fairness** of financial statements
2. **Compliance** with accounting standards (Ind AS or AS)
3. **Compliance** with Companies Act, Income Tax Act, GST law
4. **Internal controls** over financial reporting
5. **Going concern** assumption
6. **Related party transactions** disclosure
7. **Fraud risk** indicators (CARO 2020 reporting)
8. **Loan utilization** for stated purposes

## CARO 2020 reporting

Companies (Auditor's Report) Order 2020 requires auditors to comment on 21 specific matters including:
- Property, plant, and equipment maintenance
- Inventory verification frequency
- Loans and advances to related parties
- Compliance with Section 185 and 186 (loans to directors, intercorporate loans)
- Default in repayment of borrowings
- Whistleblower complaints
- Fraud reported during the year

Adverse CARO comments can affect bank financing and investor confidence.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is statutory audit mandatory for all companies?

Yes. Every company registered under the Companies Act 2013 — regardless of turnover or activity — must have its accounts audited annually by a Chartered Accountant.

### What is the difference between tax audit and statutory audit?

Statutory audit is required under the Companies Act for all companies. Tax audit under Section 44AB applies to businesses with turnover above ₹1 crore (₹10 crore if cash transactions are ≤5%) and professionals above ₹50 lakh, regardless of entity type.

### Who can conduct an audit in India?

Only a Chartered Accountant in practice, holding a valid Certificate of Practice from ICAI. Cost Accountants can conduct cost audits. CMAs can certify GSTR-9C.

### Can the same CA conduct statutory and tax audit?

Yes, the same CA can conduct both, and this is the most common arrangement.

### How often must companies rotate auditors?

Listed companies and certain large unlisted companies must rotate the audit firm every 10 years (or audit partner every 5 years) under Section 139(2).

### What is CARO and is it mandatory?

CARO 2020 (Companies Auditor's Report Order) is mandatory for most companies. It requires the auditor to comment on 21 specific matters in the audit report.

### What is ICFR / IFC?

Internal Financial Controls over Financial Reporting — required to be evaluated and reported on by auditors of all companies. Listed and large companies require formal documentation and testing.
