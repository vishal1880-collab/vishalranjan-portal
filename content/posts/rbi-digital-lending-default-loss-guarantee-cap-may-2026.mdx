The Reserve Bank of India issued the amendment to the Digital Lending Guidelines through a notification dated May 21, 2026, effective June 1, 2026, capping the Default Loss Guarantee (DLG) provided by Loan Service Providers (LSPs) at 5 percent of the underlying loan portfolio. The amendment addresses concerns that have built over FY 2024-25 and FY 2025-26 around the structural risk allocation in fintech-led embedded lending and Buy-Now-Pay-Later models.

A Default Loss Guarantee in digital lending is a guarantee provided by the LSP (typically a fintech that originates loans through a partner bank or NBFC) or any third party to absorb a defined portion of credit losses on the loan portfolio. The First Loss Default Guarantee (FLDG) is the specific subset where the LSP takes the first 5 to 10 percent of credit losses before the regulated lender takes any loss. DLGs and FLDGs have been the principal mechanism through which fintech-led embedded lending and BNPL models have allocated credit risk: the fintech, which has the customer relationship and the underwriting signals, absorbs the first losses while the regulated lender takes the residual portfolio risk.

The new 5 percent cap operates at the portfolio level. The total DLG (whether structured as FLDG, top-up guarantee, or any other form) provided by a single Loan Service Provider on a portfolio cannot exceed 5 percent of the outstanding loan amount. The cap applies regardless of how the guarantee is structured: a 3 percent FLDG plus a 3 percent second-loss guarantee would exceed the cap and is not permitted. Lenders cannot contractually stack multiple DLG arrangements with different counterparties on the same loan to exceed the 5 percent effective cap.

The documentation requirement under the revised framework is substantial. Every DLG arrangement must be backed by: a written agreement between the regulated lender and the LSP specifying the structure, percentage, trigger events, and tenor of the guarantee; a cash collateral or bank guarantee equal to the DLG amount, deposited with the regulated lender or held in an escrow arrangement; board approval at the regulated lender authorising the DLG arrangement with the specific LSP; audit committee oversight of the DLG portfolio with quarterly review; and monthly portfolio-level DLG utilisation reporting to the lender's risk management team. Verbal arrangements, side letters with embedded DLG terms, and off-balance-sheet DLGs are explicitly prohibited.

The impact on the embedded lending and BNPL models is substantial. Many fintech-led BNPL products have historically operated on DLG structures running at 10 to 15 percent of the portfolio, which provided the regulated lender with a substantial first-loss cushion and enabled the lender to extend credit at relatively thin spreads. The 5 percent cap forces a restructuring of the risk economics: the lender must either accept a higher residual credit risk (typically reflected in higher interest rates to the borrower), or the partnership structure must transition to a co-lending model.

The compliance enforcement framework is designed to surface non-compliant DLG arrangements through three channels. First, the regulated lender's statutory auditor must specifically opine on the DLG arrangements as part of the audit. Second, RBI's risk-based supervision framework will probe DLG arrangements in supervisory examinations. Third, the LSP itself, which is subject to RBI oversight as a recognised LSP under the Digital Lending Guidelines, must maintain its own records and respond to RBI inquiries on DLG arrangements.

For the regulated lender side, the principal operational action items for June 2026 are: review of all existing DLG arrangements against the 5 percent cap, restructuring of arrangements that exceed the cap, board and audit committee approval of the restructured framework, and updated documentation including collateral arrangements.
