Section 43B(h) of the Income-Tax Act, 1961, inserted by Finance Act 2023 and effective from April 1, 2024, denies expense deduction to a buyer where payment to a Micro or Small enterprise supplier is not made within the timeline specified under Section 15 of the Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Development Act, 2006. FY 2024-25 was the first year of operation of this provision, and FY 2025-26 is the second year, with the operational lessons from the first year surfacing the supplier verification workflow as the principal compliance pinch point.

The scope of Section 43B(h) is narrower than businesses often assume. Only suppliers that are Micro or Small enterprises registered under the MSME Act with valid Udyam registration are within scope. Medium enterprises are explicitly excluded. The classification thresholds under the MSME Act, after the May 2025 revision, are: Micro enterprise — investment in plant and machinery or equipment up to ₹1 crore and turnover up to ₹5 crore; Small enterprise — investment up to ₹10 crore and turnover up to ₹50 crore. The buyer must verify the supplier's MSME classification through the Udyam portal at the time of purchase, or at year-end as part of the reconciliation.

The 45-day payment rule under Section 15 of the MSME Act requires the buyer to pay the supplier within the timeline agreed in writing between the parties, subject to a statutory maximum of 45 days from the date of acceptance or deemed acceptance of goods or services. Deemed acceptance is governed by Section 2(b) of the MSME Act: if the buyer raises objection in writing within 15 days of delivery, the objection date marks the acceptance trigger; if no objection is raised within 15 days, deemed acceptance is on the date of delivery. If no written agreement on payment terms exists between the parties, the statutory default is 15 days.

The FY 2025-26 disallowance risk arises at year-end. Any payment to a Micro or Small supplier registered under the MSME Act that is outstanding beyond the 45-day window at year-end (March 31, 2026) gets the corresponding expense disallowed in the buyer's FY 2025-26 tax computation. The disallowed expense becomes deductible only in the FY of actual payment. For mid-cap buyers with substantial MSME supply chains — typical in manufacturing, retail, distribution, and infrastructure — the disallowance can range from ₹50 lakh to ₹5 crore depending on procurement volume.

The supplier verification workflow is the critical compliance dimension. Buyers should maintain a supplier master that captures, for each supplier: MSME classification (Micro, Small, Medium, or Non-MSME) at the time of last update, Udyam registration number, the date of last verification through the Udyam portal, and the contractually-agreed payment timeline. The supplier classification can change during the year as the supplier's investment or turnover crosses category thresholds, and the Udyam portal does not automatically push updates to buyers.

The year-end reconciliation playbook starts in February. For each supplier identified as Micro or Small, the buyer should reconcile: invoices outstanding as of March 31, 2026; the deemed acceptance date for each invoice; the agreed or statutory payment timeline; and the actual payment date.
